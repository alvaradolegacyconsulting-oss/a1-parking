# Managing Disputes

A dispute is when a vehicle owner (typically a resident, but sometimes a visitor or guest) believes a violation or tow was incorrect and asks for it to be reviewed. Texas Chapter 2308 gives vehicle owners specific dispute rights. ShieldMyLot™ provides workflow support for both filing and responding to disputes.

This guide covers both sides — how residents file disputes and how managers respond.

## Why disputes happen

Common reasons for disputes:

- **Wrong vehicle** — The driver towed a different vehicle than the one violating
- **Owner was authorized** — Vehicle had a permit, was on the exempt list, or was a registered resident
- **Signage problem** — Required signage was missing, damaged, or unclear
- **Identification error** — Wrong plate was recorded
- **Procedural problem** — Tow happened before required notice was given
- **Genuine misunderstanding** — Owner thought they had permission they didn't

Many of these turn out to be legitimate complaints. ShieldMyLot is designed to handle them with structured documentation, not as adversarial exchanges.

## For residents: how to file a dispute

If you believe a violation against your vehicle was incorrect:

### Step 1: Review the violation details

Log into your resident portal. Go to **My Violations**. Click on the violation in question.

You'll see:
- The violation type and description
- The location (which property, where on the property)
- The timestamp
- Photos and any video evidence
- The submitting driver's name

Take a careful look at the photos and video. Sometimes the evidence resolves the question — either confirming the violation was correct or revealing the error.

### Step 2: File the dispute

If you still believe the violation was incorrect, click **File Dispute** on the violation page.

You'll be asked:
- **Reason for dispute** — Select from common reasons or choose "Other"
- **Explanation** — Free text explaining what you believe happened
- **Supporting documentation** — Optional photos or documents that support your case

**Be specific.** "I didn't do anything wrong" is harder to evaluate than "I have a valid permit displayed on the windshield; here's a photo of it." Specifics help managers resolve disputes faster and more accurately.

### Step 3: Submit and wait for response

After submitting, your dispute is sent to the property manager for review. You'll see the dispute status in your resident portal.

**ShieldMyLot does not send emails about dispute updates.** Check your portal periodically to see status changes. Statuses include:

- **Filed** — Your dispute is in the manager's queue
- **Under review** — The manager is investigating
- **Manager response** — A response has been issued; click to read it
- **Resolved** — The dispute is closed (either upheld or in your favor)

### Step 4: Read the manager's response

When the manager responds, you'll see their reasoning, any additional evidence they considered, and the outcome. Possible outcomes:

- **Upheld** — The violation stands. The manager believes the original violation was correct.
- **Reduced** — The violation stands but a penalty (tow fee, storage charge) is reduced or waived.
- **Reversed** — The violation is voided. Any tow fees paid should be refunded; consult your manager about the process.
- **Escalated** — The manager has forwarded to the Company Admin for additional review.

### Step 5: Escalate if needed

If you disagree with the manager's response, you have options:

- **Request escalation** — Click the escalation option in the dispute response. The dispute moves to the Company Admin.
- **Texas Chapter 2308 dispute hearing** — Vehicle owners have legal rights under Chapter 2308 to request a hearing. See the law's specifics or consult an attorney.
- **Small claims court** — For tow fee disputes specifically.

ShieldMyLot is not a legal venue. The platform provides documentation that may be used in legal proceedings, but the platform itself does not adjudicate disputes legally.

## For managers: how to respond to disputes

When a resident files a dispute, it appears in your manager portal in the **Disputes** tab.

### Step 1: Review the dispute

Click on the dispute to see:
- The original violation (with all photos and video)
- The resident's explanation of why they're disputing
- Any supporting evidence they uploaded
- The dispute filing timestamp (important for Chapter 2308 timeline tracking)

### Step 2: Investigate

A thorough dispute response considers:

- **Does the photo evidence support the violation?** Was the vehicle actually in the position claimed? Is signage visible?
- **Is the plate correct?** Compare against the resident's registered vehicles. Any chance of typo?
- **Was the resident on the exempt list at the time?** Check exempt plate history.
- **Did the resident have an active visitor pass or valid permit?** Check at the violation timestamp.
- **Was signage adequate at the property at the time?** This is hard to verify after the fact, but worth considering.
- **Was the driver's action procedurally correct?** Was authorization current?

You may also want to:
- Talk to the submitting driver about what they observed
- Visit the property to see current signage
- Check the audit trail for any post-confirmation changes

### Step 3: Respond

In the dispute, click **Respond**. Fill in:

- **Outcome** — Upheld, Reduced, Reversed, or Escalated
- **Reasoning** — Your explanation. Be respectful, factual, and reference specific evidence
- **Next steps** — If applicable, what the resident should do (e.g., "Tow fee refund will be processed within 5 business days")

**Important: write the response as if it will be read by a third party** (a court, a property owner, the resident's attorney). Professional, neutral, evidence-based responses serve everyone better than emotional ones.

### Step 4: Submit

The response is logged. The resident sees the new status in their portal.

If you've reversed the violation, also:
- Coordinate with your accounting team for any fee refunds
- Update the violation record (use the post-confirmation tools described in [Tow Tickets and Evidence](../enforcement-track/07-tow-tickets-and-evidence.md))
- Note the outcome in any external reporting you do

## Response timelines

Texas Chapter 2308 includes specific timeline requirements for disputes. Generally:

- Vehicle owners have **14 days** from a tow to file a dispute
- Operators must respond within a defined window (specific timing depends on Chapter 2308 sections)

Your published response timeline (set in [Account Setup](../getting-started/02-account-setup.md)) should match or be shorter than what Chapter 2308 requires.

If you consistently miss timeline requirements, you may face Chapter 2308 penalties and lose disputes by default.

## Disputes audit trail

Every action on a dispute is logged:

- When the dispute was filed
- When it was viewed by managers
- All response drafts and final responses
- Any escalations
- The final outcome
- Any post-resolution changes

This trail is critical if a dispute escalates to legal proceedings. Don't try to alter or hide dispute history — it can't be done, and trying to would create legal exposure.

## Common questions

**A resident filed a dispute on a violation I'm certain was correct. How should I respond?**
With evidence and respect. Show them what the driver observed (photos, video, time stamp). Explain the rules they violated and how the signage communicated those rules. Don't be defensive — just be factual.

**Can I refuse a dispute outright?**
You can uphold the violation, but you cannot refuse to engage with the dispute. Chapter 2308 requires you to respond. Uphold-without-engagement is not a valid response.

**What if a resident files a dispute that seems frivolous?**
Respond professionally regardless. Frivolous-seeming disputes are still legal filings. A respectful, evidence-based response — even to a clearly wrong dispute — is the right path.

**A resident wants to talk on the phone. Should I?**
Sure, if you prefer phone over written exchange. But also document the conversation in writing (notes in the dispute response) so the audit trail is complete. Phone conversations alone aren't sufficient documentation.

**Can a resident dispute a violation without a tow happening?**
Yes. Any violation can be disputed regardless of whether a tow occurred. The dispute workflow doesn't depend on tow status.

**What if the dispute reveals an error by the driver?**
Reverse the violation. Use the post-confirmation tools to manage the violation record (don't delete it — the record stands with the reversal noted). If this is a pattern, address it with the driver through performance management. One-off errors are normal; a repeating pattern signals a training need.

**Can I see disputes filed against my company across all properties?**
Yes, if you're a Company Admin. Manager view is filtered to your assigned properties. Admin view shows all properties.

## Best practices for dispute prevention

The best dispute is one that never needs to be filed. To minimize disputes:

- **Train drivers thoroughly on signage requirements** — Most disputes that succeed are signage-related
- **Document evidence completely** — Photos of plate, vehicle, location, and signage in every violation
- **Use video for ambiguous cases** — Video resolves "what was actually happening" disputes
- **Maintain accurate exempt plate lists** — Don't tow vehicles that should be exempt
- **Communicate with property owners** — Some disputes arise from owner-driver misalignment on what to enforce
- **Audit your team's submissions** — Spot-check violations; drivers who skip steps generate more disputes

A well-run enforcement operation has disputes, but they're rare and usually resolve in favor of the operator because evidence is strong.
